Solar System

Milky Way Galaxy

The solar system is a fascinating and intricate celestial arrangement that consists of the sun, planets, moons, asteroids, comets, and various other celestial objects. At the heart of our solar system is the sun, a massive, blazing star that provides the energy and warmth necessary for life on Earth. Orbiting around the sun are eight planets, including Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune. Each planet has its own unique characteristics and plays a vital role in the dynamics of the solar system.

Space, the vast and mysterious expanse that extends beyond Earth's atmosphere, has long been a subject of fascination and wonder for humanity. It represents the final frontier, a realm where the laws of physics and the boundaries of human knowledge are continually tested. In the emptiness of space, there is no atmosphere, no sound, and no up or down. It is a place of extreme temperatures, from the scorching heat of the sun to the frigid cold of deep space. The study of space, known as astronomy, has deepened our understanding of the cosmos and our place within it. It has sparked philosophical questions about the origins of the universe, the existence of extraterrestrial life, and the nature of black holes and dark matter.

Wonders of Space

Astrology, the age-old study of the stars and their influence on human life, has captivated civilizations for centuries. In astrology, the positions and movements of celestial bodies, especially planets, are believed to shape our personalities, destinies, and even our daily lives. Each planet in our solar system is associated with unique qualities and characteristics, and astrologers use these planetary influences to create birth charts and horoscopes. Whether you're a believer or a skeptic, the fascination with how the planets align and impact our earthly existence is a testament to our enduring curiosity about the cosmos. As we gaze at the night sky and ponder the mysteries of the universe, we find a connection between the celestial and the terrestrial, a reminder that we are part of a grand cosmic dance.

The sun

The Sun is the biggest object in our solar system, with a distance of 695,508 kilometres from centre to surface. It contains 99.86% of the mass of the entire solar system and could contain roughly 1.3 million Earths.The Sun is an average-sized star. Some stars are just a tenth of its size, while others are more than 700 times bigger. Due to its huge mass and strong gravity, the Sun is a near perfect sphere.The core is the hottest part of the Sun , at 15 million degrees Celsius. This is hot enough to sustain thermonuclear fusion, when hydrogen atoms are fused together to form larger helium atoms. This releases an extraordinary amount of energy which in turn is released as heat and light. The energy produced at the core takes up to a million years to reach the outer layer known as the convective zone. At this point the temperature drops to around two million degrees Celsius. By the time it gets to the surface the temperature is down to a much cooler 5,973Â°C - but it's still hot enough to boil diamonds. In the Sun's atmosphere, known as the corona, the temperature begins to rise again to roughly two million degrees Celsius. As distance from the core of the Sun grows wider, the temperature would be expected to drop. This dramatic increase in temperature in the atmosphere is one of the star's biggest mysteries.

Mercury

Mercury is the smallest and closest planet to the Sun in our solar system. It is a terrestrial planet, meaning it is made of rock and metal like Earth. Mercury is also the fastest planet, orbiting the Sun once every 88 Earth days. Mercury has a heavily cratered surface, similar to the Moon. This is because Mercury has no atmosphere to protect it from impacts. Mercury also has a very thin exosphere, which is made up of hydrogen, helium, and oxygen atoms. Mercury's extreme temperature variations are one of its most notable features. The side of Mercury facing the Sun can reach temperatures of up to 800 degrees Fahrenheit (430 degrees Celsius), while the side facing away from the Sun can get as cold as -300 degrees Fahrenheit (-184 degrees Celsius) Mercury has no natural satellites and no rings. It is also the densest planet in our solar system, with a density of 5.43 grams per cubic centimeter. This is because Mercury has a large metallic core, which makes up about 70% of its mass.

Jupiter

Jupiter is the fifth planet from the Sun and the largest in the Solar System. It is a gas giant with a mass more than two and a half times that of all the other planets in the Solar System combined, and slightly less than one one-thousandth the mass of the Sun. Jupiter orbits the Sun at a distance of 5.20 AU (778.5 Gm) with an orbital period of 11.86 years. Jupiter is the third brightest natural object in the Earth's night sky after the Moon and Venus, and it has been observed since prehistoric times. It was named after Jupiter, the chief deity of ancient Roman religion.

On December 21, 2020, Jupiter and Saturn met in a "great conjunction," the closest they could be seen in the sky together for nearly 800 years. A conjunction occurs when two or more planets line up exactly, and when Jupiter and Saturn align, their vibrations blend and work together. Jupiter is particularly bright, appearing as a brilliant point of white light in the sky, while Saturn is a fainter dull white object, yet still about as bright as a typical bright star. Great conjunctions occur approximately every 20 years when Jupiter "overtakes" Saturn in its orbit. They are named "great" because they are by far the rarest of the conjunctions between naked-eye planets (excluding Uranus and Neptune). As successive great conjunctions occur nearly 120Â° apart, their appearances form a triangular pattern, and every third conjunction returns after some 60 years to the vicinity of the first.

Neptune

Neptune is the eighth and farthest planet from the Sun. It is the fourth-largest planet in the Solar System by diameter, the third-most-massive planet, and the densest giant planet. It is 17 times the mass of Earth, and slightly more massive than its near-twin Uranus. Neptune is denser and physically smaller than Uranus because its greater mass causes more gravitational compression of its atmosphere. Being composed primarily of gases and liquids, it has no well-defined solid surface. The planet orbits the Sun once every 164.8 years at an orbital distance of 30.1 astronomical units (4.5 billion kilometres; 2.8 billion miles). It is named after the Roman god of the sea, representing Neptune's trident.

Black-hole

A black hole is a region of spacetime where gravity is so strong that nothing, including light and other electromagnetic waves, has enough energy to escape it.[2] The theory of general relativity predicts that a sufficiently compact mass can deform spacetime to form a black hole.The boundary of no escape is called the event horizon. Although it has a great effect on the fate and circumstances of an object crossing it, it has no locally detectable features according to general relativity. In many ways, a black hole acts like an ideal black body, as it reflects no light.Moreover, quantum field theory in curved spacetime predicts that event horizons emit Hawking radiation, with the same spectrum as a black body of a temperature inversely proportional to its mass. This temperature is of the order of billionths of a kelvin for stellar black holes, making it essentially impossible to observe directly.
